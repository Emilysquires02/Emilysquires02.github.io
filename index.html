Are students worse effected by the rent crisis?

Aims

After 3 years of university I know all too well about the yearly struggle of trying to find housing. Finding an adequate property as a student with a minimal budget seems impossible at times. It is highly popular in the UK for students to move away for university and therefore, this creates a high demand for student properties. In this project I want to outline the extent of this problem, the reason for it and any possible solutions.

Firstly, enrolment into tertiary education has largely increased over the past 50 years. We can break this down more recently by looking at the number of students at the UK's largest universities we can see that all the universities increased their student intakes over the 6 years, other than Leeds Beckett and the University of South Wales. This illustrates a growing demand for rental properties in areas surrounding a university.

Despite the increasing demand from students for rental properties and prices increasing above trend levels the number of rental houses available has been slowing in growth in recent years according to provisional data from the ONS.

Chart7

Overall, I believe students are the worse effected by the rent crisis. Students are in the areas where there is increasing demand and no new supply of housing rents meaning rents will only continue to rise. Furthermore, as many students don’t have jobs or they have part-time jobs they cannot compete for housing financially – making them less ideal for landlords.

It looks like the student rental problem is unlikely to be solved without Government intervention because of the rising rent prices, increase in student intake from universities and falling frowth in rental properties

To look into this further, it would be interesting to compare certain area’s in more detail – such as comparing the North West and South West.
